Date: 2026-05-02
Epic: 2
Facilitator: John (Product Manager)
Participants: Product, Developer, QA, Operations

## Epic Review Summary

- Epic status: complete
- Stories complete: 3/3
- Story keys:
  - 2-1-inbound-normalization-and-idempotency
  - 2-2-ai-reply-contract-and-failure-handling
  - 2-3-outbound-delivery-retry-and-fallback
- Retrospective scope: pipeline reliability, AI failure contract correctness, retry/fallback determinism, observability coverage, and lessons for downstream hardening

---

## What Went Well

1. **The expiring-store seam held cleanly across the full epic.** `create_expiring_store()` with the `seen_recently` / `clear` / `close` interface gave normalization and idempotency a single stable boundary. Downstream stories (Epic 5 teardown hardening, Epic 6 retry contract tests) re-used it without modification.

2. **AI reply contract was made explicit with typed outcomes.** Converting `openai_service` from exception-throwing to controlled failure states (`success`, `timeout`, `auth_error`, `rate_limited`, `provider_error`) gave the rest of the pipeline — fallback logic, operator review flagging, escalation detection — a dependable decision surface with no uncaught exceptions reaching the route.

3. **Retry-and-fallback policy was implemented deterministically at the correct layer.** Backoff delays of 1, 2, 4 seconds with a maximum of three attempts plus a single deterministic fallback send landed in `send_message()` in `whatsapp_utils.py`, matching PRD FR5 exactly and becoming the anchor for subsequent contract tests in Epic 6.

4. **Correlation ID propagation from route to outbound delivery was preserved.** Passing `request_id` into `handle_message()` and through to `send_message()` meant all retry and fallback log entries were traceable to the originating inbound request from the start.

5. **Structured return contract from `send_message()` unified downstream state.** `ok`, `status`, `fallback_sent`, `operator_review_flagged`, and `attempts` gave the webhook orchestrator, dashboard message log, and release-gate tests a consistent output shape that required no reconciliation across epics.

6. **Store backend fallback-to-memory resilience was implemented and tested early.** `STATE_STORE_FALLBACK_TO_MEMORY=true` safeguard ensured SQLite init failures degraded gracefully rather than blocking the webhook path, matching Architecture Decision 2.

---

## What Did Not Go Well

1. **Retry semantics were partially implicit in the codebase before explicit contract testing.** While the retry policy existed in `send_message()`, the fail-then-recover sequence (transient failure followed by eventual success within retry window) was not locked as a CI contract until Epic 6, Story 6.1. This was flagged in the Epic 5 retrospective and addressed, but should have been a Story 2.3 acceptance criterion.

2. **Outbound recipient correctness depended on a convention, not a guard.** AC1 required sends to target `wa_id` from the originating inbound message, but this was an implicit routing assumption rather than an explicit validation at the send call site. Incorrect recipient routing would have been a silent user-facing bug.

3. **Deferred background delivery observability was incomplete at Epic 2 closure.** Deferred delivery terminal outcomes (success/failure in background threads) did not bind a stable correlation ID in the worker context. This left a gap where deferred path logs showed `corr=n/a` and structured log buffers missed stable correlation fields. The fix was delivered in Epic 6, Story 6.4 — but the design decision should have been an explicit AC in Story 2.3.

4. **Store `close()` lifecycle integration relied on app teardown discipline.** Resource teardown worked when the app factory was used correctly, but the seam was not validated explicitly in tests until later stories. A missing `close()` call in a test or alternate startup path would have silently leaked connections.

5. **Fallback send and operator-review flag were not verified as an atomic pair in early tests.** The design required both behaviors to occur on retry exhaustion, but initial tests validated them independently, not as a joint invariant. This was caught during release-gate construction but created a brief window of incomplete coverage.

---

## Root Causes

1. **Retry contract was treated as an implementation detail rather than a release-blocking correctness concern.** Without an explicit fail-then-recover test contract in Story 2.3 acceptance criteria, the behavior existed but was not machine-verified to behave correctly under varied transient-failure timing.

2. **Outbound recipient routing was a shared assumption not a guarded invariant.** The `wa_id` routing decision was spread across `process_whatsapp_message()` and `send_message()` without an explicit parameter validation or contract at the boundary.

3. **Background delivery was architected without an explicit observability requirement.** The deferred path was treated as an implementation optimization rather than a separate observability domain requiring its own correlation and terminal-outcome spec.

4. **Store lifecycle was tested only indirectly through integration flows.** Without an explicit teardown integration test early, the `close()` contract depended on developer discipline and app-factory correctness without automated safety net.

5. **Acceptance criteria did not treat fallback + operator-review as an atomic postcondition.** The PRD required both, but story acceptance criteria listed them as separate bullet points, making it natural to test them independently rather than together.

---

## Concrete Process Improvements Identified

1. Add a fail-then-recover contract test as a required acceptance criterion for any story that implements retry/backoff behavior, not as a deferred hardening step.
2. Treat outbound recipient routing as an explicit parameter-validation boundary with a test asserting the correct target for every outbound send path.
3. Require explicit terminal-outcome observability (structured log + artifact with correlation ID) as an acceptance criterion for any background or deferred delivery path.
4. Add a teardown lifecycle integration test for any store or extension that exposes `close()` at the time it is first introduced.
5. Write joint postcondition tests (both outcomes must be true simultaneously) for any acceptance criterion that specifies two behaviors as a required pair (e.g., fallback sent AND operator review flagged).

---

## Continuity Notes

- Epic 2 delivered a functionally complete inbound-to-reply pipeline that held through three subsequent hardening epics without fundamental restructuring.
- The retry/fallback policy was sound at implementation time but lacked machine-verified contract enforcement, which had to be retrofitted in Epic 6. The lesson: behavioral contracts for reliability-critical paths must be locked in the same sprint they are built, not deferred.
- The structured return contract from `send_message()` and the typed AI result from `openai_service` were the two most reusable decisions from this epic — downstream stories in Epics 4, 5, and 6 consumed both without modification.
